Stage-linkage
and its limits

Parkinson's disease vs. schizophrenia.

Some diseases announce themselves years before they arrive. Others don't. The difference isn't the science. It's the architecture.

Two disorders. One framework. A striking asymmetry.

The central question

When does a biomarker actually explain a disease?

A biomarker associated with a diagnosis may reflect a causal process, or something more peripheral. Most candidate biomarkers establish group-level associations without revealing which of these categories applies.

Contributory

The biomarker tracks a mechanism that amplifies pathology already underway. Upstream of progression, causally implicated.

Compensatory

A biological response to pathology, present because of the disease and not driving it. Tracking it can mislead if mistaken for a cause.

Consequential

Tracks severity without influencing it. A state marker. Real and measurable, but not where the disease starts.

This essay asks

What distinguishes a biomarker that stages a disease from one that merely rides alongside it? Parkinson's disease and schizophrenia are examined as contrasting cases.

Evaluative lens

Three criteria for stage-linkage

Each criterion is progressively harder to satisfy. Satisfying all three raises evidence above group-level association without establishing causation. These criteria are a conceptual tool for grading evidence, not a formal scale.

01 / Mechanism
Plausible mechanism

The biomarker identifies a credible biological mechanism linked to the disorder, not just a statistical correlation.

02 / Detection
Cross-stage detection

Detectable at relevant illness stages, including prodromal states where possible and not only at diagnosis.

03 / Tracking
Longitudinal tracking

At its strongest, the biomarker tracks or precedes clinical change over time, predicting disease progression rather than merely accompanying it.

03 · Parkinson's disease

A biomarker that stages.

Parkinson's disease offers what stage-linkage requires: a candidate molecular pathway and a clinically recognisable prodromal phase in which that pathway can be tracked before diagnosis. Among the molecular processes implicated in Parkinson's disease, α-synuclein aggregation is the focus here. It is the only pathway with a biologically validated in vivo biomarker integrated into a research staging system.

Key evidence
General reading

A test can detect Parkinson's disease hiding in the body years before any motor symptoms appear, before the person has any idea. It works by detecting misfolded protein in cerebrospinal fluid, not by measuring symptoms.

Research detail

α-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA) demonstrated prodromal sensitivity in participants with REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) and hyposmia prior to motor onset. Present in ~86% of enriched prodromal cohort.Siderowf et al. (2023), Lancet Neurology · 87.7% sensitivity, 96% specificity in established Parkinson's disease.

The test doesn't just detect Parkinson's disease; it can distinguish it from similar-looking conditions with near-perfect accuracy, even in patients not yet diagnosed.

Research detail

Bernhardt et al. (2025): quantitative Lewy-fold-specific SAA distinguished Parkinson's disease-associated alpha-synucleinopathy from multiple system atrophy (MSA) with 100% specificity in 170 participants.Bernhardt et al. (2025), Acta Neuropathologica.

More recently, researchers showed the test can predict how quickly someone will decline, not just whether they have the disease but how it will progress.

Research detail

Orrú et al. (2025): SAA kinetic measures predicted clinical progression including cognitive decline in 1,631 participants. Bernhardt et al. (2025): quantitative Lewy-fold scores increased alongside clinical worsening over time.Orrú et al. (2025), Lancet Neurology.

Caveat · LRRK2 subtype

SAA positivity drops to 34.7% in LRRK2 gene mutation carriers with normal olfaction, compared to ~88% in the broader cohort. Since LRRK2 mutations are the most common monogenic cause of Parkinson's disease, stage-linkage applies to a biologically defined subtype, not to Parkinson's disease as a whole clinical entity.

04 · Schizophrenia

A pathway that converges.

In schizophrenia, the strongest in vivo biomarker evidence concerns elevated presynaptic striatal dopamine. It is a credible mechanism in established illness, but it occupies a structurally different position. As a "final common pathway," it reflects the convergent endpoint of multiple upstream trajectories. A biomarker positioned downstream of diverse aetiologies cannot distinguish between the trajectories that converge on it.

Non-specific prodrome
Anxiety, depression, sleep disturbance, social withdrawal. Can appear 2 to 10 years before first-episode psychosis (FEP). Heavily overlaps with depression and anxiety disorders. No biological anchor. Faded here because it is not a reliable biomarker signal.
−0 yr before first-episode psychosis
Clinical high risk for psychosis (CHR-P) window
Attenuated psychotic symptoms, identified with a specialist interview. Mean duration: 21.6 months before first-episode psychosis (FEP). 36% of CHR-P individuals develop psychosis within 3 years; 64% do not convert. No equivalent of the α-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA) exists for schizophrenia.

The dopamine signal: what happens when you look closer.

Elevated striatal dopamine is the strongest in vivo finding in established schizophrenia. The effect is real and meaningfully large across 21 positron emission tomography (PET) studies in one landmark meta-analysis.

The structural problem

A biomarker positioned downstream of diverse aetiologies cannot distinguish between the trajectories that converge on it. Stage-linkage assumes a process with a traceable origin. A final common pathway offers none, making sequential detection across stages structurally implausible rather than merely undemonstrated.

06 · Synthesis

Architecture determines the ceiling.

The asymmetry between the two programmes reflects their position within each disorder's causal architecture, not investigative effort. Where pathology converges from multiple upstream origins, the most informative finding may not be a staged mechanism but the demonstration that no single mechanism organises the disorder.

Conclusion

A disorder's causal architecture sets the ceiling on what biomarker research can demonstrate. Where pathology converges from multiple origins, the most informative finding may be that no single mechanism organises the disorder.
