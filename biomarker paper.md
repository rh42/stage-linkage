# Stage-Linkage and Its Limits: What In Vivo Biomarker Research Reveals About Parkinson's Disease and Schizophrenia

Aetiopathogenesis refers to the biological processes that drive a disorder: what they are, when they emerge, and how they relate to illness course. In vivo biomarker research makes it possible to investigate these processes in living patients, rather than through postmortem analysis or clinical observation alone. But biomarker findings vary in what they actually track. A biomarker associated with a diagnosis may reflect a causal process or something more peripheral. It may be contributory, amplifying pathology already underway; compensatory, reflecting a biological response to it; or consequential, tracking severity without influencing it. In practice, most candidate biomarkers establish group-level associations without revealing which of these categories applies (Abi-Dargham et al., 2023). This essay argues that biomarker research advances understanding of aetiopathogenesis when it connects a plausible biological mechanism to identifiable stages of illness course. Parkinson's disease (PD) and schizophrenia are examined as contrasting cases: in PD, a biomarker closely tied to the disorder's core molecular pathology is available; in schizophrenia, it is not.

## Evaluative Lens

A biomarker advances understanding of aetiopathogenesis when it satisfies three criteria: (1) it identifies a plausible biological mechanism; (2) it is detectable at relevant illness stages, including prodromal states where possible, not only at diagnosis; and (3) at its strongest, it tracks or precedes longitudinal clinical change. The criteria are ordered by evidential strength: cross-sectional detection across stages supports stage-linkage, while prospective tracking is a more demanding test. Satisfying all three criteria raises the evidence above group-level association without establishing causation. This framework is a conceptual tool for grading evidence, not a formal scale. The question applied to each disorder is: what mechanism is being probed, at what stage, and with what evidence that the biomarker maps disease course rather than merely accompanying diagnosis? The two cases are examined against these three criteria in turn, and the asymmetry in how each disorder meets them is itself a finding about disease architecture.

## Parkinson's Disease

PD is examined first because its biology supplies what stage-linkage requires. It offers a candidate molecular pathway and a clinically recognisable prodromal phase in which that pathway can be tracked before diagnosis. Among the molecular processes implicated in PD, α-synuclein aggregation is the focus here, because it is the only pathway with a biologically validated in vivo biomarker integrated into a research staging system (Simuni et al., 2024). The prodromal phase is biologically legible: REM sleep behaviour disorder, reduced olfaction, and autonomic dysfunction can each precede motor onset by years to decades (Postuma & Berg, 2019). This pre-diagnostic window allows biomarkers to be evaluated against disease course rather than diagnostic status alone, directly testing the stage-linkage framework. Recent work has formalised this approach in the Neuronal α-synuclein Disease Integrated Staging System (NSD-ISS), which uses the distribution of α-synuclein pathology to define biological stages of neuronal synuclein disease in research cohorts (Simuni et al., 2024).

The α-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA) detects misfolded α-synuclein in cerebrospinal fluid, satisfying the first criterion by identifying the candidate mechanism in living patients. In the Parkinson's Progression Markers Initiative cohort, Siderowf et al. (2023) reported SAA sensitivity of approximately 88% and specificity of 96% in established PD. The assay also detected positivity in prodromal participants with REM sleep behaviour disorder or reduced olfaction before motor diagnosis, satisfying the second criterion: the molecular abnormality is present before the clinical threshold. Bernhardt et al. (2025) strengthened the case for criterion 1 using a quantitative SAA variant that distinguished Lewy-fold α-synucleinopathies from multiple system atrophy with 100% specificity in 170 participants, confirming that the assay detects α-synuclein in its PD-associated conformation rather than generically.

The third criterion, longitudinal tracking, is where the newer evidence concentrates. Bernhardt et al.'s (2025) quantitative Lewy-fold pathology score correlated with motor severity and cognitive impairment, and in longitudinal tracking of seven PD patients, scores increased alongside clinical worsening. Orrú et al. (2025) provided a larger-scale test: in 1,631 participants, SAA kinetic measures predicted clinical progression including cognitive decline. This is the strongest current evidence that SAA tracks rather than merely accompanies disease. α-Synuclein aggregation is therefore best read as, at minimum, primary contributory: its detection before motor onset rules out a purely downstream role, though whether it initiates neurodegeneration or principally amplifies an earlier process remains unresolved (Simuni et al., 2024).

Even where the three criteria hold, the staged model they support is not the whole of PD. SAA positivity drops to 34.7% in LRRK2 carriers with normal olfaction, compared to approximately 88% in the broader PD cohort (Siderowf et al., 2023). Since LRRK2 mutations are the most common monogenic cause of PD (Blauwendraat et al., 2020), this sharp drop suggests a distinct pathobiological subtype in which α-synuclein aggregation may not be the primary driver. Stage-linkage therefore applies to a biologically defined subtype rather than to PD as a clinical entity. Even within the synuclein-positive majority, the NSD-ISS remains a research framework: no disease-modifying treatment has yet been validated against its stages, so its therapeutic promise is still prospective rather than realised (Simuni et al., 2024). PD therefore comes closest to satisfying all three criteria within the limits of its synuclein-positive majority, but this is stage-linkage of a mechanism, not yet demonstration of sufficiency for disease progression.

## Schizophrenia

In schizophrenia, the strongest in vivo biomarker evidence concerns elevated presynaptic striatal dopamine. Other systems, including cortical glutamatergic function, have also been implicated, but dopamine is the only mechanism for which a substantial PET literature in established illness exists (McCutcheon et al., 2018). A meta-analysis of 21 PET studies, including 269 patients and 313 controls, reported a medium-to-large effect size (Hedges' g = 0.68, p < .001), making dopamine a credible mechanistic candidate in established illness (McCutcheon et al., 2018). The first criterion, identification of a plausible biological mechanism, is met. However, the same literature characterises striatal dysregulation as a "final common pathway": a shared downstream endpoint onto which neurodevelopmental, environmental, and genetic inputs all converge (Howes & Kapur, 2009). The implications for stage-linkage follow directly. A biomarker positioned downstream of diverse aetiologies cannot distinguish between the trajectories that converge on it. Stage-linkage assumes a process with a traceable origin; a final common pathway offers none, making sequential detection across stages structurally implausible rather than merely undemonstrated. The available clinical staging landmarks, such as clinical high risk (CHR), first-episode psychosis, and established illness, are recognisable, but they are clinically rather than biologically anchored. They do not carry the same epistemic weight as PD's prodromal window. This structural feature, not the quality of the evidence, determines what the dopamine literature can demonstrate.

The test of the second criterion, whether the mechanism is detectable before diagnosis, comes from the CHR literature. McCutcheon et al. (2021), in a meta-analysis of 48 studies including 1,288 CHR participants and 1,187 controls, found no significant overall difference in striatal presynaptic dopamine between groups, suggesting that, at the group level, elevated striatal dopamine is currently not a reliable feature of the CHR state. Moreover, a meta-regression from the same study found that CHR–control differences decreased over time (estimate = −0.06, 95% CI −0.11 to −0.007, p = .025). The authors interpret this as evidence that earlier, smaller studies overestimated the effect, a pattern consistent with publication bias in a maturing literature. Howes et al. (2020), in a longitudinal PET study of 51 CHR individuals, found that dopamine synthesis capacity predicted symptom worsening (r = .35, p < .05) but did not predict transition to psychosis (p = .22). In a sample of this size, that null does not rule out a modest effect, but it is inconsistent with dopamine acting as a primary driver of transition.

A biomarker that tracks dimensional symptom burden but not the categorical transition to illness is functioning as a consequential state marker, not as a driver of stage progression. In PD, the pathology-proximal biomarker was detectable before clinical diagnosis; here, the candidate mechanism shows no reliable elevation in the equivalent pre-diagnostic population. Criterion 1 therefore survives: dopamine remains a credible mechanism in established illness, but the CHR evidence does not extend it backwards. The second criterion is only weakly met, and longitudinal tracking, the third criterion, is not satisfied at the level of the stage transition the framework most directly tests.

If dopamine cannot anchor onset, the next test is whether it organises illness course consistently after diagnosis. Evidence from treatment-resistant schizophrenia (TRS) shows it does not. In a meta-analysis of variance across 65 studies (983 patients, 968 controls), Brugger et al. (2020) confirmed a medium-to-large overall effect on dopamine synthesis capacity (Hedges' g = 0.65, p = .004); in the treatment-resistant subgroup, however, synthesis capacity did not differ significantly from controls (p > .3), indicating the overall signal is carried entirely by non-resistant patients. This is a further failure of criterion 3, now in the post-diagnostic direction, rather than a separate kind of problem: dopamine fails to track illness course forward, just as it failed to anchor it backward.

A polygenic PET study points in the same direction: Eisenberg et al. (2025) found that in 187 medication-naive healthy adults with no psychiatric diagnosis, TRS-specific polygenic risk scores predicted lower dopamine synthesis capacity — while general schizophrenia risk predicted higher — locating the difference in biological predisposition rather than in illness chronicity or antipsychotic exposure. TRS is not a marginal subgroup: Diniz et al. (2023) estimated its prevalence at approximately 37% in a meta-analysis of 50 studies and nearly 30,000 patients. Together, these findings reveal schizophrenia's non-unitary architecture: dopamine neither anchors onset nor organises illness course consistently. In PD, staged progression held across the disorder, with subtype exceptions confined to a defined genetic minority. In schizophrenia, the subtype exception is the central finding. The dopamine programme illuminates aetiopathogenesis, not by staging a mechanism, but by exposing that no single mechanism organises the disorder.

## Synthesis

The difference in outcomes between the two programmes reflects their position within each disorder's causal architecture. Synuclein-positive PD meets all three criteria, whereas schizophrenia meets only the first. α-Synuclein aggregation is the pathologically defining feature of PD: it is what the biological staging system is built around, and its molecular detection therefore maps onto illness stage rather than merely accompanying diagnosis. Dopamine dysregulation occupies a structurally different position in schizophrenia. As a final common pathway, it reflects the convergent endpoint of multiple upstream trajectories and cannot recover the stages at which those trajectories diverge. In PD, α-synuclein aggregation is best read as primary contributory: upstream of clinical progression and correlating with its pace, though whether it initiates or principally amplifies neurodegeneration is not yet resolved. In non-resistant schizophrenia, striatal dopamine functions as a state marker, tracking illness rather than driving it.

Both programmes also expose limits that are not specific to either disorder but to observational in vivo biomarker research itself. Biological staging systems order abnormalities and predict clinical milestones across cohorts, but they do not demonstrate that the staged process is sufficient to drive progression. Longitudinal SAA studies show that kinetic measures predict decline without resolving whether the assay tracks a mechanism that is necessary and sufficient for progression. Subgroup heterogeneity sharpens the point from the opposite direction: LRRK2-related PD falls outside SAA-based staging, and TRS falls outside dopaminergic models of schizophrenia, so neither biomarker captures its disorder universally. Within the observational in vivo evidence reviewed here, moving from association and staging to claims of causal sufficiency remains out of reach.

## Conclusion

In vivo biomarker research advances understanding of aetiopathogenesis most convincingly when it connects a plausible biological mechanism to identifiable stages of illness course, but the two case studies show that what this understanding reveals depends on disease architecture rather than investigative effort. In PD, a pathology-proximal biomarker has yielded a biologically staged model in which α-synuclein SAA satisfies criteria for mechanism identification, cross-stage detection, and emerging longitudinal tracking, albeit with important subtype and evidential limits. In schizophrenia, striatal dopamine has instead brought heterogeneity to the foreground: as a final common pathway, it reflects convergent rather than staged causation, and dopamine dysregulation in non-resistant illness behaves as a consequential state marker, while the TRS subgroup sits outside the model entirely. Across both disorders, current in vivo data can locate a biomarker's role more precisely than group-level association allows, without yet settling questions of sufficiency. What the two cases jointly show is that a disorder's causal architecture sets the ceiling on what biomarker research can demonstrate. Where pathology converges from multiple upstream origins, the most informative finding may not be a staged mechanism but the demonstration that no single mechanism organises the disorder.
