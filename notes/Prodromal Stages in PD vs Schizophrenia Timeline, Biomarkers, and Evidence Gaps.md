# Prodromal Stages in Parkinson's Disease vs Schizophrenia: Timeline, Biomarkers, and Evidence Gaps

## Purpose

This document is prepared to support an interactive explainer comparing prodromal staging in Parkinson's Disease (PD) and Schizophrenia (SCZ) for a mixed peer/public audience. It verifies the proposed PD timeline, constructs the equivalent SCZ prodromal timeline, evaluates biological marker evidence in each disease, and identifies where the SCZ biomarker gap is most acute. The PD vs SCZ comparison is structurally asymmetric: PD now has a validated biological anchor in αSyn-SAA; SCZ does not. That gap is the analytical centre of this document.

***

## Part 1: Verification of the PD Prodromal Timeline

The user-proposed timeline is largely accurate, but some nuances deserve flagging.

### REM Sleep Behaviour Disorder (RBD): ~15–20 years before motor onset

**Verdict: Broadly accurate, but the outer bound requires qualification.**

RBD is the most specific single prodromal marker for alpha-synucleinopathy. A landmark prospective study by Postuma et al. (Brain, 2019) traced evolution from idiopathic RBD to diagnosed neurodegeneration and found that olfactory loss — the earliest non-motor marker — had a predicted onset over 20 years before phenoconversion, while frank motor symptoms developed well after RBD onset. A 2024 review confirms that both RBD and olfactory deficits are the most important prodromal non-motor markers. However, the 15–20 year figure refers to conversion from *diagnosed* RBD to *diagnosed* PD, not from the biological start of RBD. Since RBD itself is often undiagnosed for years, the true biological window may extend further. Conversion from RBD to overt synucleinopathy runs at roughly 6–7% per year, yielding a median phenoconversion around 12–14 years in prospective cohorts, with a substantial minority taking longer.[^1][^2][^3]

### Hyposmia: ~10 years before motor onset

**Verdict: Plausible but imprecise — olfactory loss may precede motor onset by more than 10 years in some data.**

The Brain 2019 data places olfactory loss as the *first* feature to develop, with predicted onset **>20 years** before phenoconversion in that cohort — earlier than RBD in that particular analysis. A 2025 Revue Neurologique review states a range of 2–36 years, with an average of ~10 years before motor symptom onset, drawing on large case-control data. The "~10 years" figure is probably best described as a median estimate from population-level data; for the specific sub-population with iRBD + hyposmia, the prodromal window is substantially longer. For the visualisation, the ~10 year mark is defensible as a population average but should carry a note that it can be earlier.[^4][^1]

### Autonomic Dysfunction: ~8 years before motor onset

**Verdict: Approximately correct for urinary dysfunction specifically; constipation may appear earlier.**

The Brain 2019 data shows that constipation and erectile dysfunction begin approximately 10–16 years before phenoconversion, while *slight urinary dysfunction* begins 7–9 years before. The 8-year anchor is therefore a reasonable estimate for urinary autonomic dysfunction but understates when GI/autonomic features first appear if the user means constipation.[^1]

### αSyn-SAA Detectability: ~5 years before diagnosis

**Verdict: Broadly accurate but mechanistically more complex than the timeline implies.**

The original PPMI Lancet Neurology 2023 study demonstrated αSyn-SAA sensitivity of 87.7% in diagnosed PD and **86%** positivity in prodromal participants (those with RBD + hyposmia). A 2025 Neurology study extended this: αSyn-SAA positivity and faster seed amplification kinetics (shorter time-to-threshold, TTT) in prodromal PD individuals predicted phenoconversion, with log-rank p = 0.001 for TTT as a predictor. A comprehensive 2025 meta-analysis across biomatrices found overall sensitivity 86%, specificity 92%, with CSF, skin, blood, and extracellular vesicles achieving ≥90% accuracy. A 2024 skin-specific SAA achieved 92.46% sensitivity and 93.33% specificity in 332 PD patients vs 285 controls.[^5][^6][^7][^8]

The "~5 years before diagnosis" framing for SAA detectability is somewhat misleading in a different direction: SAA positivity has been demonstrated in **prodromal** individuals years before *motor* diagnosis, but the assay is not primarily useful as a countdown-to-diagnosis biomarker. Rather, it functions as a biological classifier distinguishing alpha-synucleinopathy from non-synucleinopathy causes of prodromal symptoms — a categorical test, not a staging tool. It is nonetheless the most analytically robust fluid biomarker in neurodegeneration today.

| Feature | Approximate onset before motor diagnosis | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Olfactory loss (hyposmia) | >10 years (mean), up to >20 years in iRBD cohorts | May be the earliest detectable marker[^1] |
| GI/autonomic (constipation) | ~10–16 years | Often subclinical[^1] |
| REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) | ~7–15+ years | Strongest single predictor; 74% of RBD meet MDS prodromal PD criteria[^3] |
| Urinary autonomic dysfunction | ~7–9 years | More specific window[^1] |

## Part 2: The Schizophrenia Prodromal Framework — Constructs and Timeline

Unlike PD, where the prodromal timeline extends decades and can now be anchored biologically, the SCZ prodrome is measured in **months to a few years** and is defined entirely through clinical and behavioural criteria. The absence of a comparable biological anchor is the single most important structural difference for the visualisation.

### The Four Constructs: UHR, ARMS, BLIPS, and Schizotypal/Basic Symptoms

These are not discrete sequential stages but **operationalised risk criteria** used in different research traditions. They overlap considerably.

**Ultra-High Risk (UHR) / Clinical High Risk (CHR-P):** This is the dominant contemporary framework, developed by Yung, McGorry and collaborators. Individuals meet UHR criteria if they have one or more of: (1) attenuated psychotic symptoms (APS) of sufficient frequency and severity; (2) brief limited intermittent psychotic symptoms (BLIPS) that self-resolve in <1 week; or (3) genetic risk plus functional decline. Conversion to psychosis in prospective cohorts is approximately 15–35% over 3 years, with considerable variation by site and era. Earlier studies reported higher conversion rates (40–50% at 1–2 years); more recent estimates are lower (12–21%), likely due to ascertainment effects and help-seeking behaviour changes.[^9][^10]

**At-Risk Mental State (ARMS):** This is the terminology used primarily in UK/Australian early-intervention services and largely overlaps with UHR. It is a clinical umbrella that may not meet full UHR criteria. ARMS individuals may convert to psychosis or remit; it is not a staging diagnosis per se.

**Brief Limited Intermittent Psychotic Symptoms (BLIPS):** BLIPS are one of the three UHR inclusion criteria. They represent transient, spontaneously remitting frank psychotic symptoms lasting <1 week. A Schizophrenia Bulletin 2016 study found an overall 5-year psychosis risk of 54% for BLIPS, with recurrent episodes carrying a hazard ratio of 3.98 compared to mono-episodic BLIPS. BLIPS with seriously disorganizing or dangerous features carried a 5-year psychosis risk of 89%. This is qualitatively different from APS — BLIPS sit at the border of prodrome and diagnosable psychosis, not years before it.[^11]

**Basic Symptoms / Schizotypal Spectrum:** The German "basic symptoms" tradition (Huber, Klosterkötter) identified subtle subjective anomalies in cognition, perception, and affective experience — what Huber called "coenaesthesias" — that can precede UHR-level symptoms by years. A Klosterkötter (2001, JAMA Psychiatry) prospective study showed that basic symptoms predicted schizophrenia with a sensitivity of 0.98 and specificity of 0.59 over a mean 9.6-year follow-up. Two clusters are particularly predictive: cognitive-perceptual disturbances (thought interference, perceptual distortions) and interpersonal sensitivity. This tradition suggests that the detectable onset of *some* SCZ-trajectory individuals may begin 5–10+ years before psychosis, but the predictive validity at the individual level remains insufficient for clinical action at that distance.[^12]

**Schizotypal Personality Disorder (SPD):** A small proportion of individuals with SPD convert to schizophrenia — approximately 10–15% in long-term follow-up. SPD should not be routinely mapped as a "prodromal stage" because most individuals with SPD do not convert, and most individuals who develop schizophrenia do not pass through diagnosable SPD. It is better understood as a partially overlapping liability spectrum.

### Prodromal Duration: What NAPLS Data Show

The most rigorous prospective estimate of prodrome duration comes from NAPLS-2. Among 94 CHR-P participants who converted to psychosis, the mean prodrome duration (from CHR-P syndrome onset to first psychosis) was **21.6 months**, with a "rule of thirds": roughly one-third converted within 12 months of CHR-P onset, one-third between 12 and 24 months, and one-third beyond 24 months. This is critically important for the visualisation: the **clinically detectable and operationalised prodrome is approximately 6–36 months** in most converters, not years. The longer "basic symptoms" horizon of 5–10 years represents a *pre-CHR* phase that is detectable only in retrospect or in research settings using specialist instruments.[^13][^14]

### Schizophrenia Prodromal Timeline for Visualisation

| Phase | Approximate window before first-episode psychosis (FEP) | Key features |
|---|---|---|
| Premorbid neurodevelopmental deviation | Years to decade+ before FEP | Subtle cognitive and social deficits detectable in childhood (mean age for social withdrawal: 11.1 years, psychosis: 13.2 years in paediatric-onset samples)[^15] |
| Non-specific prodrome | ~2–10 years before FEP | Anxiety, depression, sleep disturbance, social withdrawal, functional decline; often misdiagnosed[^16] |
| Basic symptoms (Huber/BS stage) | ~3–10 years before FEP (in minority identified prospectively) | Subjective cognitive-perceptual anomalies: thought interference, reduced energy, derealisation[^12] |
| UHR/CHR-P window | ~6–36 months before FEP (mean ~21 months) | Operationalised APS, BLIPS, or genetic risk + decline; 15–35% convert in 3 years[^13][^10] |
| BLIPS | Weeks to months before or at FEP | Transient frank psychosis; 5-year FEP risk 54–89%[^11] |

## Part 3: Behavioural and Cognitive Changes — What Is Reliably Observed and When

### Cognitive Decline

Cognitive impairment is detectable before psychosis, but it is not specific and does not show a clean temporal signature equivalent to hyposmia or RBD in PD. Premorbid IQ is lower in individuals who later develop schizophrenia, with declines in processing speed and verbal learning detectable in the CHR phase. A meta-analysis of neurocognition in prodromal psychosis (JAMA Psychiatry 2012) confirmed moderate deficits across multiple domains at CHR-P. Crucially, cognitive impairment in the prodrome is neither sensitive nor specific enough to serve as a standalone risk marker — it overlaps heavily with depression, ADHD, and other conditions.[^17][^18]

In the **UHR phase** specifically (the operationalised 6–36 month window), deficits in processing speed, visual learning, and social cognition are reliable findings. Working memory and verbal memory decline further at conversion. Semantic fluency is particularly sensitive: deterioration predicts non-remission while improvement is associated with remission.[^19][^18][^20]

### Social Withdrawal and Negative-Symptom Trajectory

Social withdrawal begins years before psychosis. In a paediatric-onset sample, mean age for social withdrawal onset was **11.1 years**, cognitive decline **11.9 years**, and psychosis **13.2 years** — but this was a high-severity sample not representative of all-comers. For most individuals, social and functional decline accelerates in the UHR window (months before psychosis) rather than years before.[^15]

### Subthreshold Positive Symptoms (APS)

Attenuated positive symptoms — including referential thinking, magical thinking, perceptual distortions, and difficulties with reality testing — characterise the UHR window. These are by definition proximate to the conversion threshold. In retrospective studies of first-episode patients, attenuated symptoms are present approximately 1–2 years before FEP, while non-specific anxiety and affective symptoms precede this by additional months to a few years.[^21][^9]

## Part 4: In Vivo Biomarkers in the SCZ Prodrome — Reliability Assessment

This is where the contrast with PD is sharpest. The field does not have an equivalent of αSyn-SAA for schizophrenia: no single biomarker has achieved sufficient sensitivity, specificity, and independent replication to anchor the prodromal diagnosis biologically.

### Neuroimaging: Structural MRI

Longitudinal MRI data from NAPLS show that CHR-P individuals who later convert to psychosis display accelerated gray matter thinning in the superior and medial prefrontal cortex (PFC) and ventricular expansion. Ventricular enlargement and cortical thinning are temporally linked rather than independent processes in the prodromal window. A 2023 meta-analysis confirmed broad cortical thickness reduction in schizophrenia, particularly in frontal and temporal regions. However, the predictive value of structural MRI at the *individual level* is modest — effect sizes are meaningful at the group level but insufficient for clinical use. Conversion to psychosis cannot be predicted reliably from a single MRI scan in a CHR individual.[^22][^23]

### Neuroimaging: Striatal Dopamine (PET)

Pre-synaptic striatal dopamine synthesis capacity (measured with [18F]DOPA PET) is elevated in UHR individuals compared to healthy controls, and a landmark 2011 study showed that striatal dopamine synthesis *further increases* longitudinally as UHR participants convert to psychosis. This is mechanistically coherent — aberrant salience driven by hyperdopaminergia is central to the dominant theory of positive psychotic symptoms. The correlation between CAARMS prodromal symptom severity and striatal Ki value (r = 0.48, p = .02) is significant. However, PET is not a scalable clinical biomarker: it is expensive, involves radiation, and shows considerable within-group variability. More recent multimodal PET/MR studies in CHR populations have failed to replicate early PET findings in larger samples.[^24][^25][^26]

### Neuroimaging: MRS Glutamate

Magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS) studies of glutamate in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and thalamus have attracted considerable attention based on the NMDA hypofunction model of schizophrenia. Elevated GLX (glutamate + glutamine) is observed in early-stage illness and the prodrome in some studies, and ACC glutamate levels correlate with sensory integration deficits and neural network connectivity in schizophrenia patients. However, the published literature is highly inconsistent: a 2024 systematic review and 2026 meta-analysis found no significant overall difference in ACC GLX between schizophrenia patients and controls, with significant differences emerging only in treatment-resistant subgroups. At the CHR-P level, MRS findings are even more heterogeneous. MRS glutamate is not a reliable prodromal biomarker in the current evidence base.[^27][^28][^29][^30]

### Electrophysiology: Mismatch Negativity (MMN) and P300

Among all candidate SCZ prodromal biomarkers, **mismatch negativity** (MMN) has the strongest and most replicated evidence base. MMN is a pre-attentive auditory ERP generated by deviance detection (an auditory oddball paradigm). MMN amplitude is reduced in established schizophrenia and in CHR-P populations. A 2023 MEG study (n=270) confirmed MMN reductions across SCZ, FEP, and CHR samples with no significant diagnosis-by-amplitude interaction — suggesting MMN reduction is a transdiagnostic marker of psychosis risk rather than schizophrenia-specific.[^31]

A 2025 systematic review of 29 EEG studies comparing CHR converters vs non-converters found that MMN and P300 ERPs, along with elevated resting-state theta and delta power, showed the highest consistency in predicting conversion. However, findings were overall mixed; P50 sensory gating did not emerge as a reliable predictor. The reviewers explicitly noted that inconsistency in clinical and neurophysiological measures across studies limits translation, and called for larger standardised multi-centre trials.[^32][^33]

A large MMN study with 580 CHR individuals found MMN amplitude deficits sensitive to future psychosis conversion. The 2025 Frontiers study confirmed this but emphasised that reliability across paradigms and laboratories remains a challenge.[^34][^35]

**Assessment:** MMN has sufficient evidence to be considered the strongest electrophysiological candidate biomarker in CHR populations. It is not clinically actionable as a standalone test, but in combination with clinical criteria it adds moderate predictive value. The P300 adds incremental information. Resting-state EEG theta power is promising but less replicated.

### Fluid Biomarkers: Inflammatory and Proteomic Markers

This is the weakest part of the SCZ prodromal biomarker evidence base.

**Cortisol / HPA axis:** Elevated basal cortisol has been reported in CHR-P individuals, but only CHR *converters* show significantly elevated basal cortisol and higher stressor-cortisol concordance compared to both non-converters and healthy controls. The finding is statistically modest (confidence intervals overlap between converters and controls) and not clinically actionable.[^36]

**Kynurenic acid (KYNA) and inflammatory markers:** A 2024 study of antipsychotic-naïve first-episode psychosis patients found substantially elevated serum KYNA and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) compared to healthy controls, with higher baseline KYNA predicting poor response to antipsychotics. However, this is a FEP sample, not a CHR-P sample. Peripheral KYNA dysregulation in the prodrome (before FEP) has not been convincingly established.[^37]

**Plasma proteomics:** A 2024 multi-cohort study (NAPLS-2, NAPLS-3, NEURAPRO; total n=754) examined plasma proteomic prediction of transition to psychosis in CHR individuals. The a priori-specified model yielded a C-statistic of **0.51** (barely above chance). Complement proteins C8B, C4B, C5, and LRG1 showed weak group-level associations with transition, but none survived FDR correction for multiple comparisons. The study explicitly did not confirm earlier, smaller proteomic prediction findings. Previous positive findings from smaller samples should be interpreted with caution.[^38]

**AMP SCZ (Accelerating Medicines Partnership® Schizophrenia):** This is the most ambitious current effort to address the fluid biomarker gap. The programme will follow ~2,000 CHR and ~640 community participants for two years, collecting blood, saliva, genomics (polygenic risk scores), and salivary cortisol at baseline and 2-month follow-up. It uses a discovery-based proteomic platform and is designed to test whether combinations of biomarkers — rather than any single marker — can improve prediction. The first data outputs from this programme are anticipated from 2025 onwards.[^39][^40][^41]

## Part 5: Direct Comparison — Where the Biomarker Evidence Stands

| Domain | Parkinson's Disease | Schizophrenia | Gap Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Validated fluid biomarker | αSyn-SAA: 86–99% sensitivity (CSF/skin/blood), highly specific for synucleinopathy[^5][^7] | None validated at individual level | **Fundamental asymmetry** — PD has a molecular anchor; SCZ does not |
| Neuroimaging (structural) | DAT-SPECT for dopaminergic deficit; well-validated | Gray matter thinning in PFC/superior temporal gyrus at group level; low individual-level predictive power[^22][^23] | SCZ imaging is descriptive, not diagnostic |
| Neuroimaging (functional/molecular) | Cardiac sympathetic scintigraphy in MDS criteria | Striatal [18F]DOPA PET: elevated at UHR, increases at conversion; not scalable[^24][^26] | PD has validated DAT imaging; SCZ PET is a research tool only |
| Electrophysiology | Not clinically used prodromally | MMN/P300: most consistent ERP predictors; moderate effect sizes, mixed replication across sites[^32][^31][^35] | SCZ has the more developed electrophysiology literature, but translation to clinical use is unresolved |
| Cognitive biomarkers | Subtle cognitive decline 7–9 years before PD motor onset | Processing speed, verbal memory deficits at CHR-P; non-specific[^18][^20] | Neither disease has a cognitive biomarker with actionable specificity |
| Timeline certainty | High: retrospective and prospective data converge on a ~15–20 year window[^1][^2] | Moderate: retrospective data suggest 2–10 year non-specific phase; prospective CHR-P data cover ~6–36 months[^13] | SCZ prodromal window is shorter and clinically operationalised only in its final ~2 years |
| Conversion rate from confirmed prodrome | ~80–90% phenoconversion from iRBD at 20 years (long-term follow-up)[^2] | ~15–35% CHR-P to psychosis within 3 years[^10][^42] | Far more SCZ "prodromes" do not convert — false positive problem is much larger |

## Part 6: The Central Gap and What It Means for the Visualisation

The core asymmetry is not merely that SCZ lacks an SAA equivalent. It is more fundamental:

1. **The SCZ prodrome lacks a biological mechanism with peripheral detectability.** PD pathology (αSyn aggregation) is present in the gut, skin, and peripheral nervous system years before central motor involvement, which is why peripheral biofluid assays work. In SCZ, the posited pathological processes (dopamine dysregulation, NMDA hypofunction, synaptic pruning aberrations) are primarily central and are not known to generate peripherally detectable molecular signatures with sufficient specificity.

2. **The conversion problem.** Approximately 65–85% of CHR-P individuals do not convert to schizophrenia. Many remit. This means any biomarker in the CHR-P window faces not only sensitivity but fundamental specificity challenges. Even a biomarker with 80% sensitivity and 80% specificity in converters vs non-converters would produce a large false-positive burden when applied to a low base-rate population. PD's αSyn-SAA, by contrast, is applied in populations already enriched by objective prodromal markers (RBD + hyposmia), with a much higher pretest probability.[^43][^10][^42]

3. **The timeline mismatch.** PD's prodromal window is measured in *decades*. The clinically operationalised SCZ prodrome is measured in *months*. This creates a different intervention logic entirely. The early-detection prize in SCZ is identifying individuals in the basic-symptoms phase (5–10 years before psychosis) — but the tools to do so are specialist instruments used in research settings, not scalable clinical biomarkers.

4. **2023–2025 developments that narrow the gap (tentatively).** The AMP SCZ programme is the most important recent development. It is not yet producing biomarker outputs but will be the largest CHR biomarker study undertaken. The EEG literature (MMN, P300, theta power) is producing the most consistent electrophysiological findings. The proteomic literature has, if anything, moved *backwards* with the 2024 multi-cohort null result for plasma proteins. Neuroinflammatory markers (complement, IL-1β-mediated kynurenine pathway) remain plausible candidates but have not been validated in prospective CHR-P studies.[^33][^35][^40][^44][^41][^32][^39][^38]

## Notes on Accuracy and Inference Limits

- PD timeline figures (RBD, hyposmia, autonomic dysfunction) are drawn from prospective cohort data (predominantly iRBD cohorts) and are not uniformly applicable to all PD subtypes. Body-first vs brain-first subtypes show different non-motor trajectories.[^45][^46]
- The NAPLS prodrome duration estimate (mean 21.6 months) applies only to individuals who *converted* to psychosis and were retrospectively identified as CHR-P — it does not capture individuals in a pre-CHR phase.[^13]
- The Klosterkötter basic symptoms data (sensitivity 0.98, specificity 0.59) is from a highly enriched clinical sample followed 9.6 years; these figures should not be applied to general population screening.[^12]
- MMN effect sizes in CHR converters vs non-converters are significant but moderate; no single EEG measure achieves clinical-grade sensitivity/specificity for individual prediction.[^32]
- The AMP SCZ biomarker paper (Perkins et al., 2025) describes design and rationale only — it is not yet a results paper.[^39]
