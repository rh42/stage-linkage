Parkinson's Disease (PD) — How Early Can We See It Coming?

Scroll through — the left rail tracks where in time you are. Events fade in as you read. Scroll back — the marker retreats.

Parkinson's disease (PD)
−20 yr
−20 yr before motor diagnosis
Olfactory loss (hyposmia)
First detectable signal. Often dismissed as age or allergies. Can begin more than 20 years before motor onset. By −13yr: constipation and colour vision changes also emerge, often subclinical.
−11 yr before motor diagnosis
REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD)
The body acts out dreams during sleep — kicking, talking, reaching. The strongest single predictor of phenoconversion to Parkinson's disease (PD). By −9yr: subtle motor signs and urinary dysfunction also appear.
−6 yr before motor diagnosis
α-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA) — detectable
A test detects misfolded α-synuclein protein in cerebrospinal fluid. Present in ~86% of enriched prodromal cohorts. Not a countdown — it identifies the type of pathology, not a date.
−2 yr before motor diagnosis
Bradykinesia, rigidity and tremor emerge
Cardinal motor symptoms appear in sequence: bradykinesia (slowness) first at ~−5yr, then rigidity at ~−3yr, tremor at ~−2yr. The biology has been present for over a decade by this point.

Clinical Parkinson's disease (PD) threshold. The biology started long before this moment.

Schizophrenia (SCZ) — The Same Question, a Different Answer

Section is intentionally shorter — fewer events, shorter time window. The asymmetry in section length mirrors the asymmetry in evidence.

Schizophrenia (SCZ)
−7 yr
−5 yr before first-episode psychosis (FEP)
Non-specific prodrome
Anxiety, depression, sleep disturbance, social withdrawal. Appears 2–10 years before first-episode psychosis (FEP). Overlaps heavily with many other conditions. No biological anchor. Deliberately faded — not a reliable biomarker signal.
−2 yr before first-episode psychosis (FEP)
Clinical high risk for psychosis (CHR-P) window
Attenuated psychotic symptoms, operationalised with a specialist interview. Mean duration: 21.6 months before first-episode psychosis (FEP). 36% of clinical high risk for psychosis (CHR-P) individuals develop psychosis within 3 years. 64% do not convert. No equivalent of the α-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA) exists.
Year 0
First-episode psychosis (FEP)
Clinical schizophrenia (SCZ) diagnosis. The operationalised prodromal window covers only the final ~2 years. There is no SAA equivalent.

of clinical high risk for psychosis (CHR-P) individuals develop psychosis within 3 years (Fusar-Poli et al., 2012; n=2,502). Contemporary multi-site cohorts report lower rates (~22%), reflecting broader ascertainment.

Component 03
The Dopamine Signal — What Happens When You Look Closer
Schizophrenia (SCZ) & Dopamine
effect size across patient groups

Elevated striatal dopamine is the strongest in vivo finding in established schizophrenia (SCZ). The effect is real, consistent, and meaningfully large across 21 positron emission tomography (PET) studies.

Then someone asked: what about the patients for whom antipsychotics don't work?

What is "g"? Hedges' g measures how big a difference actually is. Below 0.2: barely detectable. Around 0.5: moderate. 0.65 is large and consistent.

The signal you've been looking at only exists in patients for whom treatment already works. For 37% of people with schizophrenia — those with treatment-resistant schizophrenia (TRS) — dopamine isn't elevated at all.
Brugger et al. (2020): meta-analysis of variance, 65 studies, 983 patients. Overall Hedges' g = 0.65, p = .004. TRS subgroup: p > .3 vs controls. — Diniz et al. (2023): TRS prevalence ~37%, 50 studies, n ≈ 30,000.

Evidence Layer
General reading

A test can find Parkinson's disease (PD) hiding in the body years before any shaking starts — before the person has any idea.

Research detail

α-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA) demonstrated prodromal sensitivity in participants with REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) and hyposmia prior to motor onset. Present in ~86% of enriched prodromal cohort.Siderowf et al. (2023), Lancet Neurology — 87.7% sensitivity, 96% specificity.

The dopamine finding in schizophrenia (SCZ) is real — but it vanishes entirely in a third of patients. Which raises a question about what it was actually measuring.

Research detail

Brugger et al. (2020): overall Hedges' g = 0.65 across 65 studies. Treatment-resistant schizophrenia (TRS) subgroup: p > .3 vs controls. Signal carried entirely by treatment-responsive patients.Eisenberg et al. (2025): TRS-specific polygenic risk predicts lower (not higher) dopamine capacity in healthy adults.

In Parkinson's disease (PD), the biology has a traceable path going back decades. In schizophrenia (SCZ), many trajectories lead to the same endpoint — you can't run the film backwards.

Research detail

Striatal dopamine dysregulation as "final common pathway" (Howes & Kapur, 2009): a shared downstream endpoint onto which neurodevelopmental, environmental, and genetic inputs converge. Stage-linkage requires a traceable origin; convergent causal architecture structurally precludes it.
